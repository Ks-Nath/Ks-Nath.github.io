## What If Everyone Was Rich?

Let’s run a thought experiment.

Tomorrow morning, everyone wakes up rich. Not “I can order food without checking my balance” rich, but properly rich. Millions in the bank. No debt. No financial anxiety. Just financial peace and very questionable spending decisions.

At first glance, this seems like the perfect fix to every economic problem ever. Poverty disappears overnight. Everyone is comfortable. Society wins.

Unfortunately, economics also wakes up the next morning.

### Money vs Wealth (the first misunderstanding)

The first thing we get wrong is assuming money *is* wealth. It isn’t.

Money is just a medium of exchange—a tool. Real wealth is made up of goods and services: houses, food, healthcare, education, infrastructure.

If everyone suddenly has more money, but the amount of goods and services stays the same, nothing has actually become more abundant. We’ve just increased the number of people trying to claim the same pie.

This is where things start to get… expensive.

### Demand-Pull Inflation (everyone goes shopping at once)

With more money in hand, demand explodes. Everyone wants better housing, better food, better everything. Economists call this **demand-pull inflation**—when increased demand pushes prices up.

But supply doesn’t magically expand overnight. You can’t instantly build more houses or train more doctors just because people have money.

So prices rise. Fast.

Your new wealth starts losing **purchasing power**, which is just a fancy way of saying your money buys less than it did before. You’re still rich on paper, but in reality, you’re competing in a much more expensive world.

### The Wage-Price Spiral (it gets worse)

Now workers notice that everything is more expensive. So they demand higher wages. Fair enough.

Firms, facing higher wage costs, increase prices further to maintain profits.

Prices go up → wages go up → prices go up again.

This loop is called a **wage-price spiral**, and it’s exactly as fun as it sounds (which is not fun at all).

### Incentives Collapse (who’s going to work?)

Here’s a more uncomfortable question.

If everyone is financially secure, why would anyone take on difficult, stressful, or low-status jobs?

In economics, behavior is driven by **incentives**. If the financial incentive to work disappears, many people will simply opt out—especially from essential but unpleasant jobs.

This leads to a **labor supply shock**—fewer people willing to work at existing wage levels.

To attract workers, wages must rise significantly. Which again feeds into higher costs and higher prices.

Your ₹1 crore lifestyle is now competing with a ₹1 crore labor market.

### Scarcity Doesn’t Care About Your Bank Balance

Even if money becomes abundant, scarcity does not disappear.

Scarcity is the fundamental economic problem: limited resources, unlimited wants.

There is still only so much:
- Land in desirable locations  
- Time in a day  
- Skilled professionals  
- Natural resources  

No matter how rich everyone is, not everyone can have a beachfront house, the best surgeon, or the fastest-growing startup.

So competition shifts from “who has money” to “who gets access.”

Prices become a sorting mechanism again.

### Relative Wealth (the return of inequality)

Even in a world where everyone starts equally rich, differences will reappear.

Some people will invest better. Some will spend faster. Some will create value. Some will not.

Over time, **relative wealth differences** return.

Because economics isn’t just about money—it’s about decisions.

And people make very different decisions.

### Velocity of Money (why everything speeds up)

There’s also something called the **velocity of money**, which is how quickly money circulates in the economy.

If everyone suddenly has money and starts spending rapidly, the velocity increases. More transactions happen in less time.

This amplifies inflation even further.

It’s not just that people have more money—it’s that they’re using it faster.

### General Equilibrium (the system adjusts whether you like it or not)

Economies tend toward something called **equilibrium**—a state where supply and demand balance out.

When you suddenly inject massive wealth into the system, everything becomes temporarily chaotic. But over time, prices, wages, and behavior adjust.

The system stabilizes.

Just not in the way you hoped.

Instead of a universally rich society, you end up with a more expensive one where the relative structure of advantage quietly rebuilds itself.

### So What Actually Happened?

We didn’t eliminate scarcity.  
We didn’t increase real production.  
We didn’t magically create more value.

We just increased the number of people holding money.

And the economy responded exactly how it’s designed to.

### Final Thought

The idea that “everyone being rich” would solve everything sounds great—until you realize that money is only meaningful in relation to what it can buy.

If everything adjusts around it, nothing really changes.

Except the price of coffee.

Which, somehow, is still too high.
