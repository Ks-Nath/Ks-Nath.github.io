## Why Being Busy Feels Productive (But Isn’t): An Economics Explanation

I recently realized something mildly disturbing.

I can be extremely busy… and still achieve nothing of real value.

Naturally, instead of fixing this, I decided to explain it using economics.

---

### The Productivity Illusion (Now With Economics)

Economics assumes that humans are rational decision-makers. This is already a problem. Because if that were true, I wouldn’t spend 45 minutes organizing my task list instead of doing the actual task.
But let’s pretend I’m rational for a moment.

In theory, every action I take should maximize value. Every hour should be spent on the most important thing available.
In reality, I respond to messages, tweak my schedule, and suddenly feel like I’ve had a “productive day.”

This is what I like to call **low-value productivity**—it feels good, looks convincing, and achieves almost nothing.

---

### Marginal Utility (or Why the 5th Task Is Useless)

In economics, there’s this idea of marginal utility—the value you get from doing one more unit of something.

The first meaningful task you complete in a day? High value.

The second? Still good.

By the time you’re on your 15th “small task,” the value has dropped so much it’s basically emotional support at that point.

But here’s the catch—I still keep going.

Because doing something always feels better than doing the *right* thing, especially when the right thing is difficult.

---

### Diminishing Returns (a.k.a. Studying for 10 Hours and Learning Nothing)

There’s also the concept of diminishing returns.
Which basically means: doing more doesn’t always mean getting more.At some point, your effort stops translating into results.

You’ve probably experienced this while studying. The first few hours are decent. Then your brain slowly turns into mashed potatoes, but you continue anyway because stopping would feel like failure.

So you stay busy.

And convince yourself that quantity equals progress.

---

### The Real Problem: Misallocation

If an economist looked at my day, they wouldn’t say I’m lazy.
They’d say I’m allocating my resources very poorly.
My time, energy, and focus are being spent on low-return activities while high-impact work is being delayed.
Which is a polite, academic way of saying:

I’m doing everything except what actually matters.

---

### So What Now?

Unfortunately, the solution is not another system.

I know. Tragic.

The real fix is much simpler and much more annoying.

I have to identify the **highest-value** task… and actually do it first.

Not after checking messages. Not after “warming up.” Not after reorganizing my life for the 12th time.

Just do it.

**Consistently.**

Which, for reasons I cannot explain, is incredibly difficult.

---

### Final Thought

Being busy is easy. It’s comfortable. It feels productive.

But from an economics perspective, it’s often just *inefficient resource allocation*.

And once you see it that way, it’s hard to unsee.

Unfortunately.
